How it works at a glance

The system has two activities. Read these first, because everything below builds on them.

  • Gathering collects raw materials from the world (ore, hide, herbs, monster parts). Everyone can do all of it, so it is not where you specialize.
  • Production turns those materials into finished items (armor, weapons, potions, enchants). This is where you specialize, and where most of this document lives.

The whole design serves one aim: a specialty should emerge from playing, and a specialist should be clearly better than a generalist. You become "a smith" by making swords and armor, not by choosing a class.

The two activities reach that aim with opposite math, named here in plain words and detailed later:

  • Production is conserved. At any moment only one specialty is active: a small cluster of adjacent crafts, empowered to the top tiers while the rest sit on the common floor. You can learn as many crafts as you like over time and never lose what you learned, but you can keep only one specialty empowered at once, and switching which one is active has a real cost. That cost is exactly what makes a specialist worth more than a dabbler.
  • Gathering is additive. Choosing to harvest one thing better never weakens your ability to harvest anything else. Breadth is free.

The rules card. The whole production model in six lines; every section below unpacks one of them.
  • Majors: an attunement empowers 2 adjacent crafts to the legendary ceiling.
  • Hobby: plus 1 of the two crafts opposite your majors, capped at rare.
  • Floor: every other craft still crafts at common; skill is never lost, only dormant.
  • Before a first attunement: any craft can climb its tier quests to rare, which is where the game offers a title.
  • Switching: by attunement quest (a lore quest the first time, an escalating make-amends quest on a return), never by re-grinding.
  • The exception: Jack of All Trades, the breadth attunement: all ten crafts live at a mid tier, no legendary, no signature combos.
