Archetypes and attunement: two majors, a hobby, a title

Because the active specialty empowers two adjacent crafts, every specialist is defined by a pair of adjacent majors, and each of the ten adjacent pairs around the ring is a named archetype (Smith, Outfitter, Apothecary, Arcanist, and so on). On top of the majors you get a hobby: one craft from the pair sitting directly opposite your majors, empowered up to the rare tier while the rest of the opposite side stays at the floor. The rare cap is what keeps a dabbler from ever matching a real specialist and keeps high-tier crafting interdependent; and because opposite crafts are complements, the hobby doubles as a self-supply line: a Smith (Weaponcrafting plus Armorcrafting) takes Leatherworking or Tailoring, exactly the straps and cloth padding a full set of gear needs.

None of this is picked from a menu. You craft freely, your crafting trends toward an adjacent pair, and the game offers to name and attune you to it: "Smith" is a title granted for making swords and armor, not a class you chose. The system reveals itself in steps, each tied to the mechanic that triggers it:

  • Common tier: craft anything with no pressure (common is the free floor). The skill radar is visible but flat.
  • Each tier is quest-gated: the first tier quest (common to uncommon) is the moment specialties start to matter, and a one-time tutorial panel explains the cap and the radar exactly when the mechanic first bites. Attunement is a ladder, not a single event: each step deeper into an archetype is its own tier quest.
  • Two adjacent crafts climb into rare: a specialty has unmistakably emerged. The tier quest at this point grants the title, empowers your two majors, and reveals your hobby.

Switching specialties is also an attunement quest, which keeps the cost diegetic and out of the live economy's moving prices, and gives the game a natural place to spell out exactly what attuning will change on your character:

  • The first time into any given archetype, whether or not you have ever held one, is a lore quest: an earned, satisfying first step into that identity rather than a tax.
  • Returning to a specialty you previously held is a distinct repeatable make-amends quest whose cost escalates with how many times you have done it; a quest can carry anything (a time delay, a coin or material cost, more lore). Your old skill is untouched, so the cost is the quest, never a re-grind.
  • The hobby can switch too: as part of a full re-attune, or on its own, keeping your majors and taking the other opposite craft. A standalone hobby switch is expected to follow the same attunement-quest pattern at a lower cost than an archetype switch, but it must stay costly enough that flipping between the two opposites never becomes a de-facto second hobby. The old hobby goes dormant and the new one is empowered up to rare; no grind erases the first, and there is no pin or menu to manage.
